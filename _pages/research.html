Project 1: Hypervelocity Impact on Competent Rocks

5 km/s Shots Impact Crater Ejecta

This research aims to understand the behavior of brittle rocks subjected to hypervelocity impacts. We use the HyFIRE facility in HEMI at Johns Hopkins University to impact a basalt cube at 5.0 km/s with a 3 mm, aluminum sphere. In-situ diagnostics including ultrahigh speed camerates at 2 millions fps and flash X-ray are integrated to visualize the impact process. In these experiments, we are particularly interested in measuring the impact crater diameter and ejecta angle as a function of time. Our experimental results are useful for planetary science and defense communities to benchmark hydrocodes and validate constitutive models.

Project 2: Mechanism-based Constitutive Model for Competent Rocks

Microcracking Lattice Plasticity Granular flow Bulking

This project develops a mechanism-based consistutive model for competent rocks subjected to impact loading. The model incorprates elasticity, equation of state and the mechanisms shown in the left figure within a thermodynamically-consistent finite deformation framework. Compared with phenomenological constitutive models, our mechanism-based model can predict the extreme loading conditions beyond the calibrated scenarios. It has the potential to improve our understanding of geological processes and to enhance efficiency in various applications.

Project 3: Phenomenological Constitutive Model for Rocks

Damage Plasticity Pressure-dependent Rate-dependent

This work proposes a phenomenological constitutive model for rocks subjected to dynamic loading. The model considers the effects of confining pressure and strain rate on the strength and strain softening behaviors of rock. It also treats the tensile and compressive damages, both related deviatoric and volumetric deformations, seperately. Compared with the commonly used phenomenological models in commerical software, our model requires less parameters and has higher a comuptational efficiency.

Project 4: Dynamic behavior of Steel Fiber Reinforced Concrete

Constitutive model Scaling law Impact Blast

This project presents a new scaling law that explicitly accounts for matrix and fibre properties to characterize the dynamic stregnths of SFRC. We also develops a new phenomenological constitutive model to simulate the dynamic behavior of SFRC. Compared with using normal concrete material models, our model requires less calibration effort, captures SFRC behavior more accurately, and demonstrates broader applicability.

Project 5: Hydraulic Fracturing Simulation in Hydrocarbon Stimulation

3D FDEM Hydraulic fracturing Multi-stage and multi-cluster Layered tight reservoirs

This project focuses on understanding the simultaneous propagation mechanism of hydraulic fractures in hydrocarbon stimulation. We use a versatile non-planar three-dimensional FDEM method to simulate the propgation of multiple perforation clusters in a single fracturing stage. In particular, we are interested in the interaction between the hydraulic fractures propgation and the layering structure of the tight reservoir. Our research contributes to improving the productivity in stimulating shale/tight gas and shale oil.
