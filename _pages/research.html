We integrate experiments, theoritical and computational modeling, AI and AM to understand extreme events and extreme conditions. Below are our key research areas.

Project 1: Hypervelocity Impact on Competent Rocks

5 km/s Shots Impact Crater Ejecta

This research aims to understand the behavior of brittle rocks subjected to hypervelocity impacts. We use the HyFIRE facility in HEMI at Johns Hopkins University to impact a basalt cube at 5.0 km/s with a 3 mm, aluminum sphere. In-situ diagnostics including ultrahigh speed camerates at 2 millions fps and flash X-ray are integrated to visualize the impact process. In these experiments, we are particularly interested in measuring the impact crater diameter and ejecta angle as a function of time. Our experimental results are useful for planetary science and defense communities to benchmark hydrocodes and validate constitutive models.

Project 2: Mechanism-based Constitutive Model for Competent Rocks

Microcracking Damage Lattice Plasticity Granular flow

This project develops a mechanism-based consistutive model for competent rocks subjected to impact loading. The model incorprates elasticity, microcracking-induced damage, granular flow, bulking, lattice plasticity and equation of state within a thermodynamically-consistent finite deformation framework. Most of its parameters can be either measured directly from experiments or sourced from reported sub-scale simulations. Compared with phenomenological constitutive models, our mechanism-based model can predict the extreme loading conditions beyond the calibrated scenarios. It has the potential to improve our understanding of geological processes and to enhance efficiency in various applications.
